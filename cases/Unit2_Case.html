MBA301 · Unit 2 · Case Study

Airbnb — From Air Mattresses to Ideas

Ideation

© 2026 Dr. H. Haas · EU Business School

Context

San Francisco, 2007. Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia could not make rent. A design conference was coming to town; every hotel was sold out. They made a simple list: what do we have that we could offer?

The Case

The unlikely chain of analogies, re-framings and worst possible ideas behind a $75B company

The founders inflated three air mattresses in their loft, threw up a one-page website they called AirBed & Breakfast, and charged $80 a night. Three designers booked it.

For two years the idea was repeatedly declared dead. Friends told them nobody would let strangers into their home. Investors passed. Gebbia later called it the worst possible idea — which, in retrospect, was exactly why it worked.

The pivot came from an analogous domain: CouchSurfing existed, but it was a free, community-gift economy. eBay existed, but only for objects. Airbnb combined the analogy — eBay for space — and the category clicked.

Professional photography, trust ratings, and host insurance each emerged from running the cycle again: field observation (why are listings not converting?), quick prototypes, rapid validation. Each iteration was a Crazy-8-style divergence followed by ruthless convergence.

The pattern behind Airbnb is not singular genius — it is the repeated, disciplined use of ideation techniques: analogous inspiration, re-framing, worst possible idea, combinatorial thinking. The founders were designers by training. It shows.

Analysis Questions

Re-construct Airbnb's earliest idea as an analogous inspiration exercise. Which existing services did they borrow from, and what did they deliberately strip out?
The original concept was labelled the worst possible idea. Apply the NUF Test (New · Useful · Feasible) as a 2008 investor would have. What does this tell you about the limits of conventional prioritisation?
Airbnb ran the diverge-converge cycle repeatedly as the company grew. Pick one of their later innovations (e.g. Experiences, Plus). Describe how you would run a 90-minute Creative Sprint today for their next category.
