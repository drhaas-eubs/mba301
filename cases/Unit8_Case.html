Airbnb's First Deck

The Art of Pitching

© 2026 Dr. H. Haas · EU Business School

Context

2009. Airbnb (then AirBed & Breakfast) was a year-old idea repeatedly declared dead. Brian Chesky's first successful deck consisted of eleven slides. It is now one of the most studied artefacts in early-stage fundraising history.

The Case

Ten slides that raised a company — mapped to Bostelaar's Core Message protocol

Slide 1 — The name. Just the name. No tagline beyond a single descriptor: Book rooms with locals, rather than hotels. This is Bostelaar's one-sentence core message. If the audience does not grasp the venture from that sentence alone, no slide that follows will save it.

Slide 2 — Problem. Price is important. Hotels disconnect you from the city. No easy way to book with a local. Three sentences, three bullets. The decomposed concepts, force-prioritised.

Slide 3 — Solution. A web platform where users can book spaces in other people's homes. Again, compressed to a single idea.

Slide 4 — Market Validation. Couchsurfing.com had 630,000 users. VRBO had 17,000 listings in San Francisco alone. The pitch does not argue the market exists — it proves it using competitor data.

Slide 5 — Market Size. TAM, SAM, SOM. Numbered bullets. No narrative, just the arithmetic of plausibility.

Slides 6–8 — Product, Business Model, Market Adoption. Product on one slide. Business model in one sentence (10% commission). Go-to-market in three bullets.

Slides 9–10 — Competition and Advantages. A 2×2 grid of competitors, with Airbnb in the corner nobody else occupies. The advantages slide lists four reasons in 14 words total.

Slide 11 — Team. Three names. Each with a one-line credential. The ask is implied, not stated — and yet the deck raised $600,000.

The deck is notable for what it does not do: no hero's-journey narrative, no emotional appeal, no slide longer than 15 words, no jargon. It is the Bostelaar method executed with clinical discipline. Every slide is a single decomposed, prioritised claim. The message strategy is clarify, not convince — the founders assumed the investors were smart and the opportunity speakable.

Analysis Questions

Map each of the 11 slides onto Bostelaar's Distill-Decompose-Prioritise protocol. Which slide contains the single claim that, if believed, makes everything else follow?
The deck sits almost entirely on the clarify end of the message-strategy spectrum. If you had to move it 30% toward convince, which slides would you change and how?
A 2025 SaaS founder has approximately 90 seconds of investor attention before a deck is closed. Re-architect the Airbnb deck for that constraint. What survives?
