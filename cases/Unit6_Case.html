MBA301 · Unit 6 · Case Study

Sentient Technologies — Funding to Dissolution

Moving from an Idea to an Entrepreneurial Firm

© 2026 Dr. H. Haas · EU Business School

Context

Sentient Technologies was founded in 2007 by Antoine Blondeau, Babak Hodjat and Adam Cheyer. By 2014 it had emerged from stealth with $103.5 million in Series C funding from Horizons Ventures and others — one of the most heavily-funded AI companies of its generation.

The Case

$143.5 million raised across three rounds. Four offices. Three founders. Why did it end in 2019?

The founding team was exceptional. Hodjat's agent-oriented technology inspired Siri. Cheyer co-created Siri itself. Blondeau brought the business operator's discipline. All three had substantial technical reputations before Sentient began.

Funding came in three rounds: a $2m Series A, $38m Series B led by Horizons Ventures, and $103.5m Series C in 2014. Limited partnerships with Access Industries, Fosun (China) and Tata Communications (India) provided additional capital and global reach.

The firm stood up offices in San Francisco, Japan, the UK and Australia. It operated subsidiaries — Sentient Investment Management (hedge funds), Sentient Ventures (early-stage AI investments) — as well as four distinct product lines (Ascend, Aware, Invest, Science) and a custom-AI services practice.

Sentient Ascend launched in 2016 as a SaaS conversion-rate optimisation platform — a clean, high-margin, sellable category. It gained customers including Shoes.com and grew steadily. For the first time, one of Sentient's products had product-market fit and repeatable economics.

But the firm's cost structure had been built for a far larger, far more multi-product company. Burn was high. Investor expectations — after $143.5m at those multiples — demanded a scale story the Ascend business alone could not carry.

In 2019 Sentient Technologies was dissolved. Ascend was sold to Evolv. Much of the remaining IP went to Cognizant; Hodjat became Cognizant's CTO for AI. From a venture-capital perspective, this was a graceful, liquidating end — but not the outcome the $143.5m had implied.

The lessons are legible only in retrospect. The cost base, funded while the firm had multiple pre-fit products, could not be brought down fast enough once Ascend became the one that worked. The diversity of product lines diluted operating attention. And scale capital raised before category fit committed the firm to a shape it could no longer adjust.

Analysis Questions

Reconstruct Sentient's funding stages and the milestone each round implicitly promised. Where did the cap table and the milestone schedule first become misaligned?
Edmondson (2011) distinguishes praiseworthy failures from blameworthy ones. Which category does Sentient's dissolution fall into, and what specifically is worth learning from the failure?
If you had been CEO of Sentient Ascend in 2017, post-launch, what capital structure would you have argued for — and how would you have renegotiated it with a board expecting scale?
