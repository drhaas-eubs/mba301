MBA301 · Unit 4 · Case Study

Spanx — Sara Blakely and the Upside of Uncertainty

Entrepreneurial Thinking

© 2026 Dr. H. Haas · EU Business School

Context

Atlanta, 1998. Sara Blakely, 27, sells fax machines door-to-door and has $5,000 in savings. She has an idea for footless pantyhose. She has no business plan, no investors, no industry contacts, and no manufacturer willing to return her call.

The Case

$5,000, one patent, and no MBA — a textbook case of effectual reasoning

Blakely starts from means, not ends. Who am I? A persistent saleswoman. What do I know? How to cold-call. Who do I know? No one in hosiery. That is the bird-in-hand starting position.

She drives to North Carolina — America's hosiery belt — and knocks on factory doors in person. Every mill owner says no. The lemonade moment: one mill owner's daughters try on her prototype, love it, and convince their father to run a small pilot.

Her affordable loss is explicit. She had decided at the outset she could live with losing the $5,000 — and only the $5,000. Every decision is framed against that bright line. She writes her own patent application from a library book to save the $3,000 lawyers quoted.

The crazy quilt forms organically. A buyer at Neiman Marcus agrees to a meeting — Blakely pitches in person, drops her trousers to demonstrate, and walks out with an order. Oprah Winfrey's team hears of it. The network self-assembles around momentum, not plans.

Pilot-in-the-plane: Blakely refuses outside investment for 21 years. She never ceded control; the future of Spanx was always something she was making, not forecasting. In 2021 she sold a majority stake to Blackstone at a $1.2 billion valuation.

Analysis Questions

Map each of the five effectuation principles onto a specific moment in Blakely's early story. Which principle was most decisive?
Apply Knight's Risk vs Uncertainty distinction. Was Blakely acting under risk (measurable probabilities) or uncertainty (unknowable)? How does this change how we assess her choices?
A causal (ends-driven) MBA in 1998 would have written a business plan, segmented the market and pursued venture capital. What would they have produced — and would Spanx still exist today?

References

Sarasvathy, S.D. (2001) ‘Causation and Effectuation: Toward a Theoretical Shift from Economic Inevitability to Entrepreneurial Contingency’, Academy of Management Review, 26(2), pp. 243–263.

Furr, N. and Furr, S.H. (2022) The Upside of Uncertainty: A Guide to Finding Possibility in the Unknown. Boston: Harvard Business Review Press.
