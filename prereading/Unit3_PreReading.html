TitleDesign for Action
AuthorTim Brown and Roger L. Martin
SourceHarvard Business Review, September 2015

Summary

Brown and Martin argue that design thinking has been taught almost exclusively as a method for designing artefacts — products, services, experiences. But strategy and organisational change are also acts of design, and they require a different set of moves.

The core problem is intervention: a beautifully designed strategy document that nobody adopts is no strategy at all. The design of the intervention is as important as the design of the solution.

The authors present ten practices that separate effective strategy designers from strategy planners. The practices span three domains: design (projecting, prototyping, evaluating), execution (experimenting, routinizing, mobilizing, realizing, connecting), and sustainment (scaling, curating).

A critical insight: in strategy design, the artefact (the plan) and the artefact-user (the workforce) are the same system. You cannot finish one without shaping the other.

Key Framework

The Ten Practices of Strategy Design

Projecting · Prototyping · Evaluating · Experimenting · Routinizing · Mobilizing · Realizing · Connecting · Scaling · Curating. Not phases but interacting practices — leaders move between them continuously.

Guiding Questions for Your Reading

Brown and Martin criticise the conventional strategic plan as an artefact that gets handed off and then dies. In your experience, at what point in the chain does a plan typically stall?
Which of the ten practices is best-developed in your current organisation, and which is most neglected? What would it take to rebalance?
The authors argue strategy design and change management are the same discipline. If this is true, what does it change about who should be in the room when strategy is made?
