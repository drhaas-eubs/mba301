Summary

The white paper opens with a definition that separates intrapreneurship from both traditional R&D and from corporate venture capital: intrapreneurs are employees who behave like founders inside an established firm, using company resources to pursue novel business opportunities that would not survive the core organisation's usual filters.

Three building blocks structure any intrapreneurship programme: a selection mechanism that identifies the right people, a process that gives them protected time and distinct decision-making, and a governance structure that determines how successful ideas re-enter the core business.

The paper catalogues four dominant programme models: internal accelerators (like Skydeck), venture studios (company-funded vehicles building repeatedly), innovation labs (permanent R&D units), and intrapreneurship hackathons (time-boxed ideation sprints). Each suits a different corporate context.

Success factors recur across case studies: a programme design matched to the company's DNA, executive sponsorship that persists through leadership changes, clear kill criteria, and integration with — rather than separation from — the existing quality and budget processes.

Key Framework

The Intrapreneurship Building Blocks & Success Factors

Selection · protected time · governance (the three building blocks). Programme-fit · executive sponsorship · kill criteria · core-business integration (the four success factors). Missing any one is the most common cause of programme failure.

Guiding Questions for Your Reading

The white paper argues programme design must match the host company's DNA. Describe one concrete DNA trait of your own organisation, and what that implies for the programme model you would choose.
Intrapreneurs are employees pursuing opportunities the core filters would kill. What does this imply about how you would measure their performance — and whether ordinary KPIs apply?
Of the four models (accelerator, venture studio, lab, hackathon), which one fits your firm best right now? Which one fits worst — and why?
