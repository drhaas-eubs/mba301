AuthorKurt Bostelaar

Summary

Bostelaar opens with a diagnosis most pitch coaches implicitly share but rarely state clearly: the reason most business messages fail is not poor delivery but unclarified content. The speaker has not yet reduced their own idea to its essential sentence before trying to communicate it.

The playbook's three sections map the full arc. First, clarify the message strategy — decide whether you are trying to convince (motivate) or clarify (inform), knowing that every real message is a calibrated blend of the two. Second, distill the message — the Distill-Decompose-Prioritise protocol. Third, choose the vehicle — stories, visuals, metaphors, or experiential learning.

The central discipline is Distill-Decompose-Prioritise. Write a full paragraph. Extract every concept as an isolated component. Force-rank from most to least important. Re-summarise in three sentences. Finally, in one. You have not finished until you can say the one.

Vehicles matter because the core message must travel. Bostelaar is explicit that visuals outperform words for complex ideas, stories outperform logic for motivation, and metaphors outperform either for transferring understanding across domains. This is why the playbook and JD Meier's Thumbnail Thinking are natural partners — one sharpens the message, the other designs the visual vehicle.

Key Framework

Message Strategy Spectrum & Distill-Decompose-Prioritise

On one axis, the Convince ↔ Clarify spectrum tells you what proportion of your message is motivational vs informational. The DDP protocol is the operational core: paragraph → component list → prioritised ranking → three sentences → one sentence.

Guiding Questions for Your Reading

Where on the Convince ↔ Clarify spectrum does your current elevator pitch sit? Where does it need to sit?
Apply the Distill-Decompose-Prioritise protocol to a project you are currently pitching. What survives as the one sentence?
Bostelaar lists four vehicles — stories, visuals, metaphors, experiential learning. Which of these is most under-used in your own professional communication, and why?
