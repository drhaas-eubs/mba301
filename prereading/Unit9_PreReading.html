TitleWicked Problems in Design Thinking
AuthorRichard Buchanan
SourceDesign Issues, Spring 1992, 8(2), pp. 5–21

Summary

Buchanan's 1992 paper is the intellectual backbone of how design thinking engages with the most complex problems. He argues design is no longer a discipline confined to artefacts but a general approach to ill-defined, multi-stakeholder problems — what Rittel and Webber in 1973 famously called wicked.

Wicked problems have ten features: they have no definitive formulation, no stopping rule, no right-or-wrong solutions (only better-or-worse), no immediate tests of a solution, every attempt counts significantly, there is no exhaustive set of potential solutions, every wicked problem is essentially unique, every wicked problem can be the symptom of another, the choice of explanation determines the resolution, and the planner has no right to be wrong.

Buchanan extends Rittel and Webber into four orders of design: signs (communication), things (artefacts), actions (interactions, services), and thoughts (systems, environments). Most design work starts in the first two orders. Systems thinking and design thinking meet in the fourth.

The deep claim is that design thinking's real domain is indeterminacy. Every method that treats design as the application of known technique to stable problems is at best a partial description. The capacity to work productively with problems that have no clean definition is the core competence.

Key Framework

Meadows' 12 Leverage Points in a System

From lowest to highest leverage: parameters · buffer sizes · stock-and-flow structures · delays · balancing feedback loops · reinforcing feedback loops · information flows · rules · self-organisation · goals · paradigms · and the power to transcend paradigms. Most interventions operate at the bottom; the greatest leverage lies at the top.

Guiding Questions for Your Reading

Buchanan and Rittel & Webber argue wicked problems have no stopping rule. What does it mean to do good enough work on a problem that cannot be solved?
Apply Meadows' 12 Leverage Points to a wicked problem in your own industry. Which three points would you target? Where are your current interventions concentrated?
Buchanan distinguishes four orders of design. Where in the orders does your own professional work sit — and what would it take to move to the next order?
