Strategies for Learning from Failure

Moving from an Idea to an Entrepreneurial Firm

© 2026 Dr. H. Haas · EU Business School

TitleStrategies for Learning from Failure
AuthorAmy C. Edmondson
SourceHarvard Business Review, April 2011

Summary

Edmondson opens with an uncomfortable observation: organisations talk about failure as a teacher but rarely behave as if they believe it. Most firms categorise all failure as bad, punish accordingly, and therefore never surface the signals that would let them learn.

She offers a spectrum of reasons for failure — from blameworthy (deviance, inattention, inability) to praiseworthy (uncertainty, hypothesis testing, exploratory experimentation). The single biggest managerial error is treating the whole spectrum as if it belonged in one bucket.

Creating a culture that learns from failure requires three practices: detect failure earlier (especially intelligent small failures), analyse the causes systematically (without personal blame), and deliberately produce failure through experimentation — because in uncertain domains, the absence of failure is the absence of learning.

The piece is the intellectual backbone of the psychological safety agenda and a necessary complement to effectuation: entrepreneurs who apply affordable loss are, in Edmondson's terms, designing their failures in advance.

Key Framework

A Spectrum of Reasons for Failure

From deviance and inattention (blameworthy) through process inadequacy and task challenge (mixed) to hypothesis testing and exploratory experimentation (praiseworthy). Each requires a different managerial response.

Guiding Questions for Your Reading

Pick a recent failure in your organisation. Place it on Edmondson's spectrum. Was the response calibrated to its position — or was it treated more harshly (or leniently) than it deserved?
Edmondson argues some failures should be deliberately produced. What does this mean in practice, and how would you defend it to a board focused on execution excellence?
Sentient Technologies raised $143.5m and dissolved in 2019. Where on Edmondson's spectrum does this fall? What does this reveal about the vocabulary we have for venture failure?
