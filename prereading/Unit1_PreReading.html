Design Thinking

Design Thinking as Mindset

© 2026 Dr. H. Haas · EU Business School

TitleDesign Thinking
AuthorTim Brown
SourceHarvard Business Review, June 2008, pp. 84–92

Summary

Brown reframes design thinking from a stylistic profession into a strategic discipline grounded in empathy, integrative thinking, optimism, experimentation and collaboration.

The article traces the migration of design thinking from products to services to organisational strategy, arguing that innovation powered by a direct understanding of what people want is the most durable form of competitive advantage.

Brown introduces the three overlapping spaces of inspiration · ideation · implementation — not phases but interdependent activities, which is why innovation rarely proceeds in a straight line.

Crucially, Brown ties design thinking to desirability × feasibility × viability — the discipline is finding a solution that satisfies all three constraints, not just one.

Key Framework

Desirability × Feasibility × Viability (DVF)

The sweet spot of human-centred innovation. Desirability asks what people want; Feasibility asks what is technically possible; Viability asks what is economically sustainable. Breakthrough ideas live in the overlap.

Guiding Questions for Your Reading

Brown argues design thinking is a third way between pure analysis and pure intuition. What does this third way look like in a decision you have recently made at work?
How would a DVF audit of your current organisation's flagship product change the way you prioritise next quarter's backlog?
Brown describes the design thinker's personality profile — empathy, integrative thinking, optimism, experimentalism, collaboration. Which of these do you already have, and which would you need to cultivate to lead DT in your firm?
