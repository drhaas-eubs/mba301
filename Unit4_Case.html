UNIT 4 · CASE STUDY

Spanx — Sara Blakely

1998–2012Entrepreneurial Thinking

Background

Sara Blakely had $5,000 in savings, no fashion industry contacts, and no MBA when she started Spanx. What makes her a textbook effectuation case is the sequence of decisions: patented the design herself (affordable loss), used existing relationships to get the first Oprah spot (bird-in-hand), treated every rejection as a learning opportunity (lemonade), and built partnerships rather than chasing investors (crazy quilt).

Key Facts

  • Starting capital: $5,000 of personal savings
  • First product: footless pantyhose — designed on her own kitchen floor
  • Patent: wrote the patent herself (affordable loss: $750 filing fee)
  • First major distribution: Neiman Marcus — persuaded a buyer in 10 minutes
  • Breakthrough: Oprah's Favorite Things 2000 — used a networked contact (bird-in-hand)
  • Capital raise: zero external equity until 2012 private sale

Discussion Questions

  1. Map each of Blakely's early decisions to one of the 5 effectuation principles.
  2. Where in Furr's Possibility Quadrant was Spanx located in 1998? What did that imply for strategy?
  3. How did Blakely's affordable-loss commitments change after the Oprah moment? Why?
  4. Would a causal approach (writing a business plan, raising venture capital) have worked here? Why or why not?

References

  • Sarasvathy, S. (2001). 'Causation and effectuation.' Academy of Management Review.
  • Blakely, S. (2020). How I Built This podcast — NPR.
