Wicked Problems in Design Thinking

Richard BuchananDesign Issues · 1992
Key framework. Wicked Problems · Meadows' 12 Leverage Points (applied)

Key Takeaways

  1. Wicked problems have no definitive formulation — any definition is itself a political choice.
  2. Wicked problems have no stopping rule — you stop when resources run out, not when the problem is 'solved'.
  3. Every wicked problem is essentially unique, despite family resemblance to others.
  4. Design thinking is not a method for solving wicked problems — it is a discipline for staying productive inside them.
  5. Meadows' 12 leverage points provide a ranking of where intervention in a wicked system produces actual change vs cosmetic change.

Discussion Questions

  1. Is the problem you currently work on wicked? Which of the 10 properties apply?
  2. Which of Meadows' 12 leverage points are available to you at your level of the system?
  3. What would it mean to 'stop productively' on a wicked problem?
Buchanan, R. (1992). Wicked Problems in Design Thinking. Design Issues, 8(2), 5–21.  ·  Meadows, D. (1999). Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System. Sustainability Institute.
